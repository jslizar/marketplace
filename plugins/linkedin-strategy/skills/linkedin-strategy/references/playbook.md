# LinkedIn Content Playbook — What Actually Drives Performance, and What Doesn't

Distilled from Virio's research on 226,000 LinkedIn posts (April 2026).

**How to read the numbers:** every figure below means "how much bigger or smaller the effect is" versus a baseline — usually the creator's own average. All claims were tested on held-out data (236 statistically validated findings).

**Read this before anything else:** everything below describes aggregate patterns across hundreds of thousands of posts. They are tools, not rules. Use the data to make better decisions faster — and deliberately break from it when there's a specific reason to. A uniquely technical audience may reward long text-only posts; someone working through a real life event may need to write something the aggregate data says "underperforms." The data informs judgment; it never replaces it.

---

## 1. The Seven Big Takeaways

1. **Most "best practices" are illusions.** What successful creators do vs. what actually causes success are almost entirely different things. Successful creators develop distinctive writing habits *because* they're successful — copying those habits won't make someone else successful.
2. **Images help some people and hurt others.** About 40% of posts benefit from images, but 60% are actually hurt by them. "Always add an image" is wrong for most mid-size creators (10K–50K followers).
3. **Every creator cycles through four performance states** — Slump, Cold Plateau, Warm Growth, Hot Streak — like seasons. Each state needs a different content strategy. One-size-fits-all advice fails because it ignores which state a creator is in.
4. **Individual "off-brand" posts underperform, but creators who gradually evolve do better.** No sudden topic jumps; slow, steady evolution of topics over weeks and months.
5. **The *type* of reaction a post gets matters more than how many it gets.** "Insightful" reactions drive shares. "Celebrate" reactions drive volume but actually suppress sharing. These require opposite content strategies.
6. **Content choices are a real lever, but not the dominant one on any single post.** What a writer controls — format, structure, wording — accounts for about 5–6% of a post's performance on average; the rest is who the creator is, timing, and luck. But the gap between the worst and best format decisions is roughly 8×, and skilled writers close that gap consistently.
7. **14 out of 17 "universal" content rules actually reverse for different types of creators.** A tip that works for small creators often backfires for large ones, and vice versa. Personalized advice is essential.

## 2. What You Can (and Can't) Control

Across the 226,000 posts analyzed, content choices (format, structure, wording, topic) account for only about **5–6%** of a post's performance on average. That sounds like content barely matters — yet certain formats ("lead magnet" posts, "before/after 2015 vs. 2026" posts) regularly hit 3× or more their creator's normal engagement. Both are true at the same time.

### The Performance Pie (what the 5–6% actually measures)

The 5–6% is the average effect of content choices across every post in the dataset — a statistic about the *typical* post, not a cap on what's possible for any individual post. Where a post's performance comes from on average:

| Factor | How much it explains | Can you change it post-by-post? |
|---|---|---|
| Who the creator already is (reputation, audience size, history) | ~15% | No — built over months and years |
| Post-to-post randomness (timing, audience mood, luck) | ~55% | No — even identical posts vary wildly on different days |
| Things we can't observe (network effects, algorithm changes) | ~25% | No — invisible, untouchable |
| Content choices (format, structure, wording, topic) | ~5–6% | **Yes — this is the writer's lever** |

### The better frame: the gap between worst and best format choice

Averages are the wrong lens for what a writer actually does. A writer makes specific format decisions, and some are much better than others. These numbers compare each creator's format-specific posts against their *own* average (so bigger audiences aren't confused with better formats):

| Format decision | vs. creator average | What this means |
|---|---|---|
| AI-topic lead magnet | ≈ 2.74× (+174%) | The best single format decision in the data |
| Lead magnet (any topic) | +20–24% | A reliably strong lever |
| Celebratory tone | +21–29% | Strong on volume, weak on reposts |
| Story arc | +9–13% | Modest but broadly safe |
| Vulnerable tone | +10–13% | Modest lift |
| Promotional post | −6 to −10% | Small penalty |
| Endorsement / testimonial | −38 to −44% (≈ 0.34×) | The worst single format decision |

**The frame that matters:** the gap between the worst and best format decision is roughly **8×**. Going from an endorsement post (about a third of a creator's normal engagement) to an AI-topic lead magnet (about 2.7× normal) on the same creator is an enormous lift. The 5–6% number describes the average post; the 8× gap describes what decision quality is worth. A writer who consistently picks strong formats and avoids weak ones captures most of that gap.

### Why you see 3×+ on viral posts

The typical lead magnet produces a modest +7–10% lift. Most posts get modest bumps and a few go huge — the hits that dominate social proof live way up at the top. The spread across lead magnets:

| Where it ranks | vs. creator average | What it represents |
|---|---|---|
| Typical (middle of the pack) | +7–10% | The average lead magnet. Modest lift. |
| Top 25% | +55–66% | Strong performance |
| Top 10% | +170–217% | The ones that feel viral. The ones you remember. |
| Top 5% | +343–407% | Genuine outliers |

The 3×+ posts are real — they're the top 10% of lead magnets. A good format gives a better *shot* at a hit, not a guarantee. Same format, same creator, different day: wildly different results. The ~90% of performance you can't control (timing, algorithm, who's online) is still doing most of the work on any single post. The skill is (a) stacking every controllable lever so each post has its best possible shot, and (b) posting consistently enough to earn hits through good decisions and volume — not luck.

The honest framing of the number: 5–6% describes the average post, which is accurate but misleading for what a skilled writer actually does. The right frame is the 8× gap between the worst format choice (endorsements, ~1/3 of normal engagement) and the best (AI-topic lead magnets, ~2.7× normal). Closing that gap by consistently picking strong formats is where the real leverage is. The 3×+ viral posts people point to are the top 10% of hits — nobody can guarantee them, but good format decisions give a much better shot at them.

### The catch on lead magnets: comment-driven lift

The lead-magnet lift is almost entirely comment-driven. Comment rates rise **+39–49%** on lead magnets, but reactions and reposts stay roughly flat. The "comment GUIDE to get the template" mechanic generates comments, and the algorithm counts comments as engagement. Strip out comments and look at reactions alone: lead magnets have roughly zero extra lift. The format works, but through a specific mechanism — worth knowing before relying on it.

### The real levers (what actually causes better performance)

Content changes confirmed to *cause* better results, not just correlate with them:

| Content change | Typical impact | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Adding an image or carousel (when appropriate) | +46–50% | Very high |
| Using simpler, more repetitive language | +17% per step toward simpler | High |
| Adding exclamation marks | +5.6% each | Moderate |
| Avoiding question marks | Each one costs ~3.5% | Moderate |
| Spacing out posts (longer gaps) | +3–7% per doubling of gap | High |
| Opening with "I" (first-person) | +8–25% | Moderate |

## 3. Images: It's Complicated

"Should I add an image?" — the honest answer depends entirely on the creator:

| Creator type | Does adding images help? | Best format |
|---|---|---|
| Small creators (<3K followers) | Yes, strongly | Carousel (recommended 82% of the time) |
| Growing creators (3K–8K) | Declining benefit | Carousel or image |
| Mid-size creators (10K–50K) | Actually hurts performance | Text-only or selective image use |
| Large creators (50K–100K) | Neutral | Image (recommended 56% of the time) |
| Very large creators (100K+) | Slightly positive again | Image |
| New creators (1–2 posts) | Slightly negative | Text or image — avoid carousels |
| Experienced creators (22+ posts) | Positive | Carousel strongly preferred |
| Creators in a slump + low performance | Very strongly positive | Any visual format |

**Key pattern: a U-shaped curve.** Images help small creators a lot, then the benefit drops and actually turns negative around 10K–50K followers, then recovers slightly for very large creators. The creators who need images most are those in a slump with low historical performance — for them, images almost always help.

## 4. The Four Performance States Every Creator Goes Through

Every creator moves through four distinct performance states, like seasons. Recognizing which state someone is in determines the right advice at the right time.

| State | What it feels like | How long it lasts | What to do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Slump** | Most posts underperform. Only ~38% beat their baseline. | ~13 posts | Switch to images. Post less often. Try new topics. Slumps are normal and temporary. |
| **Cold Plateau** | Posts feel flat and stuck. ~40% beat baseline. | ~19 posts (hardest to escape) | Hardest state to break out of. Content tweaks alone may not work. Consider a strategic pivot in topic or audience targeting. |
| **Warm Growth** | Things are clicking. ~63% of posts beat baseline. | ~16 posts | Maintain consistency. Use images. Experiment with new angles to try to reach Hot Streak. |
| **Hot Streak** | Posts are taking off. ~86% beat baseline. | ~8–9 posts (shortest state) | Ride the wave — use media, avoid text-only. But set expectations: hot streaks always end. 81% of creators drop to Warm (not Slump) after. |

Cold Plateau is the stickiest state — once there, it's the hardest to leave. Hot Streak is the shortest — it naturally fades after about 8–9 posts. Neither fact means anyone is doing anything wrong; they're structural features of how LinkedIn works.

## 5. Five Types of Creators

| Creator type | % of creators | What they're like | Surprise finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Star Performers** | 11% | Mostly in Hot Streak. Consistently outperform. Positive trajectory. | They actually have the FEWEST followers on average (~3,900). Stars are made by content quality, not audience size. |
| **Rising Cyclers** | 18% | Bounce between Slump and Warm. Trending upward over time. | These creators need patience. The cycles are normal — focus on the upward trend. |
| **Chronically Cold** | 19% | Stuck in Slump 89% of the time. Hard to break out. | They have HIGH follower counts (~6,150). Big audiences don't prevent stagnation. |
| **Plateau Locked** | 18% | Stuck in Cold Plateau 78% of the time. Static performance. | Highest average followers (~8,100). Content optimization has limited impact here — may need a fundamental strategy shift. |
| **Steady Performers** | 33% | In Warm Growth 83% of the time. Consistent, reliable results. | The largest group. They're doing well — don't over-optimize. Focus on maintaining what works. |

**Counter-intuitive insight:** the creators with the biggest audiences are often the ones who are stuck. Lots of followers doesn't mean the content is working. Conversely, Star Performers tend to have smaller but highly engaged audiences.

## 6. Staying On-Brand vs. Evolving — The Novelty Paradox

### The contradiction

- **Individual "off-brand" posts underperform.** When a creator posts something very different from their usual topics, that specific post tends to get **~22% fewer reactions** than their typical content.
- **But creators who gradually shift their topics over time do better.** Creators who slowly evolve what they talk about see improving performance over months.

### How to resolve it

The key word is **gradual**. The creators who improve are the ones who drift slowly — expanding into adjacent topics over 10–20 posts, not making sudden jumps. Like a ship changing course: a gentle turn works, a hard swerve capsizes you.

### What "off-brand" posts actually trigger

| Reaction type | When a post is "off-brand" | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| Funny | Goes UP | People are amused by the unexpected |
| Love / Support | Goes UP slightly | Audience is sympathetic |
| Insight | Goes DOWN | Less perceived professional value |
| Celebrate | Goes DOWN | Less professional recognition |
| Like | Goes DOWN | Less default engagement |

**Practical rule:** stay in your lane for any individual post, but expand the lane slowly over time — introduce one new adjacent topic per month. Don't suddenly post about your vacation when you normally post about supply chain management.

## 7. How Reactions Actually Work

LinkedIn has six reaction types: Like, Celebrate, Love, Support, Insightful, and Funny. They are not interchangeable — each signals something different to the algorithm and to your reach.

### What each reaction does for your post

| Reaction | Effect on total reach | Effect on shares/reposts | What it signals |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Insightful** | Moderate positive | VERY strong positive | "This taught me something" — THE driver of reposts |
| **Celebrate** | Strong positive | Actually NEGATIVE | "Congratulations!" — drives volume but people don't share it forward |
| **Funny** | Strong positive | Weak | "This is entertaining" — people laugh but don't reshare |
| **Love** | Moderate | Moderate | Emotional connection |
| **Support** | Moderate | Moderate | Empathy signal |
| **Like** | NEGATIVE signal | Weak | The "minimum effort" reaction — a high % of Likes is actually bad |

### Three clusters of reactions

- **Emotional cluster:** Love + Support + Celebrate tend to appear together — "heart" reactions.
- **Intellectual cluster:** Insightful + Funny tend to appear together — "brain" reactions.
- **Default:** Like is the fallback when people don't feel strongly either way.

**Critical insight:** Celebrate and Insightful are opposites. Posts that make people say "that's smart" are fundamentally different from posts that make people say "congratulations." Wanting more shares means needing Insightful reactions, which means thought-leadership content — not achievement announcements.

### Five content strategies based on reaction patterns

| Strategy | % of posts | Typical reactions | Performance | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Thoughtful / Analytical | 19% | High Insightful | Strong reach + most reposts (12 median) | Wanting shares & thought leadership |
| Achievement / Celebration | 33% | High Celebrate | Highest total engagement | Wanting volume & visibility |
| Generic / Default | 28% | Mostly Likes | Average | Baseline — try to move away from this |
| Humor / Entertainment | 4% | High Funny | Average | Personality-driven brands only |
| Emotional / Inspirational | 16% | High Love | Lowest performance | Use sparingly — the data says these underperform |

## 8. Topics That Win and Topics That Lose

### Consistently high-performing topics

| Topic | How much it outperforms | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| New job announcements | +108% above baseline | LinkedIn's algorithm actively boosts these. Triggers Celebrate reactions at 4× the normal rate. |
| Certifications & credentials | +20% above baseline | Achievement + professional development signal |
| Excited announcements (launches, milestones) | +20% above baseline | Positive energy + news value |
| Technical expertise (e.g., AWS, cloud) | +17% above baseline | Niche authority signals |
| Gratitude posts | +9% above baseline | Warmth + community signal |

### Consistently low-performing topics

| Topic | How much it underperforms | Why it fails |
|---|---|---|
| Call-to-action posts ("join/register") | −14% below baseline | Feels like an ad. LinkedIn users scroll past promotional content. |
| Contrarian opinions ("unpopular take") | −11% below baseline | Despite popular advice to "be bold," the data shows this backfires. |
| Generic praise for others | −3% below baseline | Low-effort content doesn't stand out |
| Growth platitudes ("hustle harder") | −3% below baseline | Overused and perceived as inauthentic |
| B2B marketing / sales strategy | −2% below baseline | Oversaturated topic |
| Leadership advice (generic) | −6% below baseline | Too many people posting similar content |

**Important caveat:** topic choice explains less than 1% of total performance. HOW you write about a topic matters far more than WHAT topic you choose. A brilliant post about leadership will outperform a mediocre new-job announcement. These rankings just show the average starting advantage.

### Topic momentum

If a creator's recent post on a topic performed well, their next post on that same topic will likely perform well too — **about 70% better** than if their last post on that topic flopped. This momentum effect is strong and validated. When a topic is working, lean into it. When it's not, pivot.

## 9. Post Structure: The Basics That Matter

### The three zones of post length

| Zone | Word count | Performance | Guidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Too Short ("The Cliff") | Under ~15 words (1–2 sentences) | 12% penalty | Almost always avoid. Too short to deliver value. |
| Rising Zone | 15–100 words | Steadily improving | Each additional word helps. Good for image-heavy posts. |
| Plateau | 100+ words | Stable and strong | No penalty for going long. 500-word posts do just as well as 150-word posts. |

**The most important structural rule:** don't post anything under ~15 words. That's the single strongest threshold in the data. Beyond that, longer is generally fine — there's no "too long" penalty.

### How length interacts with format

| Format | How sensitive to word count? | Sweet spot |
|---|---|---|
| Text-only | VERY sensitive (7× more than media posts) | 150–400 words. Too short or too long both hurt. |
| Image post | Low sensitivity | Even a short caption works fine (~9 words is the peak) |
| Video post | Low-medium sensitivity | Longer captions help (400+ words) |
| Document/Carousel | Low sensitivity | Length is flexible |

### Other structural sweet spots

- **Paragraph length:** longer paragraphs outperform. Don't over-fragment your text.
- **Line breaks:** a small amount of white space helps, but excessive line breaks (the "broetry" style) slightly hurt.
- **First line length:** longer first lines (100+ characters) perform better — make the hook substantial.
- **Exclamation marks:** the sweet spot is 3–5 per post. Zero is too flat; more than 5 starts to hurt.
- **Vocabulary:** aim for a mix — use many different words, but arrange them in clear, organized patterns (lists, frameworks, repeated structures with varied examples).

**The ceiling vs. floor insight:** good structure prevents a post from flopping, but it won't make it go viral. Structure is a safety net — it protects the downside; the upside comes from the content itself.

## 10. Timing and Frequency

Timing is one of the most-asked-about topics, but one of the least impactful: it explains **less than 0.2%** of performance. Still, a few patterns are worth knowing.

### What matters (a little)

- **Spread your posts out.** If posting 3×/week, use 3 different days. This is the strongest timing finding: **+10–14%** vs. clustering posts on the same day.
- **Mornings are slightly better.** A small ~3–7% advantage, not worth rearranging schedules over.
- **Weekday vs. weekend audiences are different:**

| Day | Audience mood | Best content type |
|---|---|---|
| Tuesday | Professional / analytical | Thought leadership, insights |
| Wednesday | Achievement-oriented | Announcements, milestones, wins |
| Friday | Lighter | Humor, casual content |
| Saturday–Sunday | Personal / emotional | Inspirational stories, personal reflections |

### Best day patterns for 3 posts/week

- **Good:** Mon / Wed / Fri, or Tue / Thu / Sat
- **Bad:** Mon / Tue / Wed, or Wed / Thu / Fri (consecutive days underperform)

**Bottom line on timing:** don't obsess over it. The difference between best and worst timing is tiny compared to content quality. Spread posts across the week and post when it's convenient.

## 11. Industry and Role Differences

Content rules are not universal — what works in one industry may not work in another.

### Image effectiveness by industry

| Industry | How much images help | Takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| AI & Data Science | Strong boost | Visual content is highly valued |
| Logistics & Supply Chain | Strong boost | Images cut through text-heavy feeds |
| Marketing & Sales | Strong boost | Visual storytelling resonates |
| HR & People | Moderate boost | Helpful but not essential |
| Consulting & Coaching | Near zero | Audience prefers text/thought leadership |
| Media & Creator | Near zero | These audiences engage with ideas, not visuals |

### How different industries react

| Industry | Audience personality | What they react to most |
|---|---|---|
| AI & Data Science | Analytical | Insightful reactions dominate — they want to learn |
| HR & People | Celebratory | Celebrate reactions dominate — they cheer achievements |
| Media & Creator | Emotional / community | Love and Support dominate — personal connection |
| Logistics & Supply Chain | Functional | Mostly Likes — professional but reserved |
| Consulting & Coaching | Relational | Love and Support — relationship-driven engagement |

### Seniority differences

- Images work best for VPs and Managers.
- Video works for Senior Individual Contributors but hurts VPs and Thought Leaders.
- Thought Leaders / Creators get a natural engagement boost (**+39%**) just from their positioning.
- Industry matters more than seniority (explains 4.5% vs. 1.3% of differences).

### The one universal

Career milestone posts (new job, promotion, certification) perform well in every industry and at every seniority level. This is the only topic where industry segmentation doesn't matter.

## 12. Driving Comments Specifically

Comments are often more valuable than reactions for building relationships and demonstrating thought leadership.

### Question placement matters

- **Ending with a question: HELPS comments.** Posts that close with a question get measurably more comments.
- **Opening with a question: HURTS comments.** Starting a post with a question actually reduces comments.
- The key is *where* the question goes, not whether there is one.

### Call-to-action effectiveness

Explicitly asking people to comment ("drop a comment," "share your thoughts below") is the single most comment-specific tactic. It boosts comments without hurting reactions. Combine a closing question with a comment CTA for maximum effect.

### Comment share = good signal

Posts with a high ratio of comments to reactions actually get boosted by the algorithm. A comment-heavy post is a sign of genuine engagement, not controversy — the algorithm rewards it.

### B2B executive segment (10K–50K followers)

- Closing questions are **2.2× more effective** at driving comments for B2B executives.
- Weekend posting penalty is stronger in B2B — stick to weekdays.
- Images matter less for driving comments in B2B (they care about ideas, not visuals).
- Video actually *hurts* comments in B2B. People don't watch and comment — they scroll.

## 13. High-Frequency Posting Rules

For anyone posting 3+ times per week, specific rules become important.

### The per-post penalty

Each additional post per week costs about **2%** in per-post performance. Going from 1/week to 5/week means each individual post gets ~9% less engagement. Total weekly engagement still goes up — you're trading per-post quality for aggregate volume.

### The #1 mistake: back-to-back text posts

**Never post text-only followed by text-only at high frequency.** This is the single most avoidable mistake, costing **8–11%** engagement. Always alternate formats.

### What works at high frequency

- **Format diversity:** each additional unique format used per week gives **+3.6%** engagement. Mix text, images, carousels, and video.
- **At 5 posts/week:** format diversity gives **+4.5%** — it becomes more important the more you post.

### What hurts at high frequency

- **Questions:** each question mark costs ~4–6% when posting frequently.
- **URLs/links:** including links costs 5–8% and gets worse the more often you post.
- **Consecutive same-format posts:** especially text-to-text.

### The "anchor post" myth

There's a popular idea that alternating one "big effort" post with several "light" posts works well. The data shows this doesn't matter. What matters is **format diversity** (switching between text, image, carousel), not effort variance (alternating long and short posts).

## 14. The Two Playbooks: Steady Growth vs. Moonshot

The rules for getting consistently good engagement are actually *opposite* to the rules for creating a viral breakout post. Decide which game you're playing.

| | Steady Engagement Playbook | Moonshot / Viral Playbook |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Reliable, solid performance every time | Maximize chance of a breakout hit |
| Format | Images, carousels, documents | Text-only (higher variance = bigger potential wins) |
| Structure | Organized: line breaks, lists, structured formatting | Dense text, minimal line breaks |
| Vocabulary | Simple, repetitive, formulaic | Rich, diverse, high variety |
| Hashtags | 1–2 relevant ones | None |
| Emojis | A few for visual breaks | None |
| Hook (first line) | Strong but conventional | Bold, unusual, pattern-interrupting |
| Tone | Gratitude, positivity, specific metrics | Provocative insight, contrarian but backed by evidence |
| Exclamation marks | A few for energy | At least one, strategically placed |
| Risk level | Low — safe floor, modest ceiling | High — many will flop, but the ones that hit go big |

**Recommendation for most creators:** default to the Steady Engagement playbook for 80% of posts. Reserve the Moonshot playbook for 1 in 5 posts, when there's a genuinely unique insight to share. The worst thing to do is use the Moonshot playbook with mediocre content — you get the downside risk without the upside potential.

## 15. Applying the Findings

### 1. Always segment first

Never give blanket advice. Before making any content recommendation, establish:

- How many followers does this creator have? (<3K, 3K–50K, 50K+)
- How experienced are they? (New vs. veteran poster)
- What performance state are they in? (Slump, Cold Plateau, Warm, Hot)
- What's their historical performance level? (High, average, low)
- What industry are they in?

### 2. Detect and respond to performance states

- **In a Slump?** Switch to images, post less often, try new topics — slumps are normal and temporary.
- **In Cold Plateau?** Acknowledge it's hard. Small content tweaks won't work — consider a bigger strategic shift.
- **In Warm Growth?** Keep doing what's working. Use images. Experiment cautiously to try to reach Hot.
- **In Hot Streak?** Ride it with media-rich posts. Expect it to naturally cool off — that's normal.

### 3. Match strategy to the actual goal

Different goals require completely different strategies:

| Goal | Optimize for | Content strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Volume / follower growth | Celebrate reactions | Achievement posts, milestones, gratitude + carousels |
| Reach / reposts | Insightful reactions | Thought leadership, analytical posts, data-driven insights |
| Safety / consistent floor | Avoid flops | Carousels, structured formatting, proven topics |
| Comments / conversation | Comment-specific tactics | Closing questions, explicit CTAs, avoid video (for B2B) |

### 4. Set honest expectations

No one can promise a viral hit — on any single post, random noise dominates. What *can* be promised is consistent application of every controllable lever: better hooks, smarter format choices, sharper structure, optimal cadence, topical coherence. That's literally everything in a writer's hands. Over 50+ posts, the random noise averages out and what's left is the cumulative effect of those choices — which is large enough to separate a growing creator from a stagnant one.

### 5. Encourage gradual evolution

Don't recommend radical pivots. Guide creators to slowly expand their topics — one new adjacent theme per month, small experiments within their existing brand. The data clearly shows: small, steady evolution beats sudden reinvention.

## 16. How LinkedIn's Feed Actually Works (2026 Update)

LinkedIn rebuilt the system that decides which posts appear in people's feeds; the rebuild shipped to production in 2025–2026. The new version is meaningfully smarter in four specific ways.

### What changed

- **The feed now understands what a post is *about*, not just which words it contains.** The old system mostly matched posts to readers using keywords and hashtags. The new one understands meaning — a post about "small modular reactors" can now be shown to someone interested in "electrical engineering" even though the words don't match. Writing clearly and specifically about a topic matters more than sprinkling in hashtags.
- **The feed looks at your posts as a body of work, not as isolated one-offs.** It considers what each reader has been paying attention to over a long history of sessions and evaluates new posts against those patterns. A creator's recent posts are judged in the context of what they usually write about — consistent topic territory is rewarded.
- **The feed now measures time spent reading, not just reactions.** How long someone lingers on a post is counted as its own positive signal, separate from likes, comments, and shares. A post that holds attention gets distributed more widely even if people don't react to it.
- **When people scroll past your post, it actively hurts you.** The old system mostly ignored non-interactions. The new one uses them as a negative training signal: if your post was shown and nobody engaged, the algorithm learns to show your content less. Every scroll-past nudges the algorithm toward suppressing your future posts.

### What this means for how you write

| Shift | Old advice | New advice |
|---|---|---|
| Hashtags / keywords | Pack in relevant hashtags and keywords | Barely useful — write clearly about a specific topic instead |
| Hook (first 2 lines) | Nice to have; drives click-through on "see more" | Critical — scroll-pasts now actively hurt you, so the hook has to earn the click |
| Time spent reading | Not measured directly | A primary ranking signal — write to hold attention, not just to earn likes |
| Topic consistency across posts | Each post judged on its own | The feed sees your recent content as a body of work; staying on-topic is rewarded |
| Clickbait hooks | Sometimes worked | Backfires faster — readers scroll past bait, and that's now an active negative |

**One-sentence summary:** the new LinkedIn feed rewards content that is (1) topically clear and specific, (2) engaging enough to keep readers reading past the hook, and (3) part of a consistent body of work from the creator. It punishes keyword-stuffing and any hook that makes readers scroll past — two tactics that used to be mostly harmless and are now actively counterproductive.
