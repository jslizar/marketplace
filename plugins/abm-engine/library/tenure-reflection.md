# Tenure reflection

- **Archetype**: work-anniversary reflection at a named company, packaged as advice to the people trying to get in
- **Funnel stage**: BOFU
- **ABM mechanic**: glorifies the author's employer (or a target account the author has history with) — the company's people amplify their own; the advice frame pulls in every aspirant to that org.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: (refreshed by the scheduled library-refresh task; pinned entries never auto-replaced)
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/neilhoyne_google-lifeatgoogle-careers-activity-7321153672011214848-t5_Y — Neil Hoyne, 6,398 reactions / 365 comments, Apr 2025 (14 years at Google)
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/oduntan_ive-turned-down-a-few-acquisition-offers-activity-7292192060013432833-GHHu — Odun O., 908 reactions, Feb 2025 (turning down acquirers; customer-funded lessons)
- **Hook pattern**: "{n} years at {company}. Almost an eternity in {industry} terms." / "I've {surprising contrarian act} over the last {period}."
- **Skeleton**:
  1. Milestone or contrarian-act hook with the company named
  2. Who this is for ("the hopefuls trying to carve out your path")
  3. Arrow-blocked advice sections (2–4): each = a hard truth + the inside view that justifies it
  4. One personal story beat with specifics (the meeting, the investor, the failure)
  5. Warm close to the aspirants — invitation, not lecture
- **Rhythm & formatting**: 250–400 words; arrow-prefixed sections; conversational asides; light emoji tolerated in this format if voice allows.
- **Why it works**: tenure = authority; the advice frame converts nostalgia into utility; the employer's brand does the reach.
- **Adaptation notes**: works at anniversaries, departures, promotions — or ABOUT someone at a target account hitting a milestone (then it converges with the rise deep-dive).
- **Watch-outs**: without specifics it's a greeting card; advice must cost something to say or it reads as humble-brag.

## Top example — full post text
Source: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/neilhoyne_google-lifeatgoogle-careers-activity-7321153672011214848-t5_Y (Neil Hoyne, 6,398 reactions)

```
14 years at Google 🎂 Almost an eternity in Silicon Valley terms. ⏳ Looking back, I'm thinking about many of you who have reached out on LinkedIn, the hopefuls trying to carve out your career paths in this world. So, here's a moment to reflect and share some of my best advice for this next year... 🙂

➡️ The truth about AI: It feels like too many people - especially younger professionals - might be missing the point. While they're getting amazing with the actual tools, I see people becoming too reductive in the potential paths. Worried about replacement when they should be planning for acceleration in their careers. AI isn't coming for your economist job, your data science career or that UX gig. It's coming for the crushing grunt work that keeps you from the brilliant things that only humans can do: having an original thought, following a wild hypothesis, or connecting impossible dots into new innovations. In other words, please...please...don't be the one to limit yourself.

➡️ As for career-switchers: this might be a golden age. When AI-powered machines take over the repetitive, tedious parts of a role, like writing long emails, making slides and trudging through countless pages of research, suddenly the doors to creativity really open. I've watched people who would've sworn off ever working with a single line of syntax now diving in headfirst into vibe coding, expressing their unique view of the world through programming for the very first time. You still need the guts to cross that threshold, to think of your role more expansively.

➡️ Human connections matter more than ever. When every resume looks perfect and everyone interviews with GPT tools backing them up, people are going back to the oldest currency: who vouches for you. Did you know that half of Google's hires came from referrals at one point. Not because it's a magic ticket or something, but because great people know other great people. That's why every referral submission asks employees "How do you know this person?" As the process gets more competitive, get out there: conferences, hackathons, industry events - wherever your tribe gathers. Help others answer that question of how much they really know you. Human connection in an AI world. Isn't that a beautiful irony?

In other words, keep pushing. The road ahead will be awesome. 🛣️
```
