# Clients: voice, wedge, and ICP (TEMPLATE — fill in your own)

This is a template. Replace the sample rows with your real clients before using the skill in production. The structure matters more than the contents: for each client you need **whose voice it goes out as**, **which topics the questions pull toward (the wedge)**, and **who actually counts as ICP**.

Read this before drafting for any client. The client lens shapes **which thread to pull**. The DM itself almost never mentions the client's product, and the first DM almost never mentions the client's industry directly either. The wedge picks the topic; the soft question opens the door. The actual industry conversation happens after they reply.

**The common ground belongs to the client, not to the writer.** When writing as a named person for a client, the wedge is operator-to-operator (that person ↔ target), never the writer's own background.

**Default is always a client, never the personal brand.** If the request doesn't name a voice, ask.

### Multi-sender tie-break (when a client has more than one possible sender)

Some clients can go out as more than one person. To avoid asking every time:

1. **Set a default sender** for that client (e.g. "default to [Person 1]").
2. **Switch** to the other only when explicitly named, or when the target is clearly that person's segment/persona.
3. If a target seems materially better suited to one sender and it's a high-stakes name, surface it in one line ("this reads more like [Person 2]'s lane — want it as them?") rather than silently picking.

> _Fill in the default + the split rule for any client with multiple senders, so the skill stops guessing._

| Client | Wedge surfaces |
|---|---|

| _[Client B]_ | _[surface 1, surface 2, surface 3]_ |

If the target is far from the client's surface, that's a signal to either skip them or treat the outreach as relationship-building, not pipeline.

## 3. Per-client ICP gut-check (before drafting)

Sanity-check that the target's company is actually the kind of entity the client sells to, not just adjacent to the topic. Flag the distinction honestly when it matters — a weak fit is worth one line of pushback before you spend a draft.

For each client, write the one distinction people most often get wrong: the "adjacent but not actually ICP" trap.

- **_[Client A]_** — _Real ICP: [the entity that actually buys]. Common trap: [who feels the pain but isn't the buyer]._
- **_[Client B]_** — _Real ICP: […]. Common trap: […]._
- **_[Client C]_** — _Real ICP: […]. Common trap: […]._
- **Personal brand** — _Real ICP: [who you actually want in your network/pipeline]. Common trap: [random connections]._

**Worked illustration of the pattern (sample, not your client):** For a customs/duty-drawback client, "affected by tariffs" is *not* the same as "importer of record who pays duties and could recover them." A retailer that feels tariff pain downstream is a conversation, not pipeline; a manufacturer or distributor that imports dutiable goods and exports finished product is the real ICP. Write your own version of that distinction for each client above.

When the fit is shaky, put it in the lock line: `Writing as [client] → targeting [who] — weak ICP fit: [why]`.
