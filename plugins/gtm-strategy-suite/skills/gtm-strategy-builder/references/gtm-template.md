> ## 🧭 How to use this template — *read first, then delete this whole callout block before delivery*
>
> This is a reusable, business-model-agnostic GTM framework adapted from a best-in-class strategy report. It works for B2B SaaS, services/agencies, and physical/consumer products. Fill it in per client.
>
> **Three conventions run through the doc:**
>
> 1. **`[[ double-bracketed text ]]`** = a placeholder. Replace every one with client-specific content. (Tip: search the file for `[[` to find them all.)
> 2. **`> Quoted callouts like this one`** = guidance and instructions for *you*, the author. **Delete every callout before sending the doc to a client.**
> 3. **Regular prose, tables, and headings** = the template body. Keep the structure; edit the content.
>
> **Recommended workflow**
>
> - Don't write top-to-bottom. Do the *research-heavy* sections first (External Environment → Market Assessment → Segmentation), because every later decision (targeting, pricing, motion) depends on them.
> - **Write the Executive Summary last**, even though it appears first.
> - Not every section applies to every client. If one doesn't, either delete it or mark it `Not applicable — [reason]`. Don't leave empty placeholders in a delivered doc.
> - Each section opens with a **Purpose** line and a **How to complete** callout. Use them as the brief; replace the scaffolding with real findings.
> - **Adapt the vocabulary to the business model.** For a SaaS client "distribution" means motion/channel mix; for a CPG client it means retail/DTC/wholesale. The headers stay; the meaning flexes. Look for the `🔀 Model note` callouts that flag these swaps.
> - Cite your sources inline with bracketed numbers `[1]` and list them in **References**. A GTM doc with no sources is an opinion, not a strategy.

## 1. Executive Summary

> **Purpose:** A one-page, standalone synthesis. A busy executive should be able to read only this and know the market, the target, the offer, the price, and the plan.
>
> **How to complete (write this LAST):** Draft after every other section is done. In ~4–6 tight paragraphs, answer: (1) How big and attractive is the market, and why now? (2) Who exactly are we targeting and why them? (3) What is the offer and its single sharpest differentiator? (4) What is the price and how is it justified? (5) How will we reach, sell to, and convert these customers? (6) What outcome do we project if the plan is followed? End on a confident, specific forward statement — not "results may vary."

The `[[industry]]` market is `[[size + growth rate]]` and is positioned for `[[the core opportunity / disruption / shift]]`. `[[Client]]` holds `[[current position / share / status, or "is entering as a new player"]]`. To `[[strategic goal]]`, `[[Client]]` is bringing `[[Product]]` to market — `[[one-line description of what it is and the core innovation/value]]`.

Segmentation analysis identified `[[N]]` attractive segments; `[[N]]` were selected as the best fit: `[[primary segment]]` and `[[secondary segment]]`. Each values different attributes, and those priorities drove every downstream decision in this plan.

`[[Summarize the offer/attribute decisions and how they match each target segment's needs.]]`

`[[Summarize pricing logic and the value justification for the price point(s).]]`

`[[Summarize how the product reaches the customer: distribution/channels, the sales motion that converts demand, and the promotion that creates it.]]`

`[[Close with the projected outcome and a confident, specific statement of what success looks like if the plan is executed.]]`

## 5. Segmentation

> **Purpose:** Break the market into distinct, meaningful segments, then validate and score them so targeting is evidence-based — not a guess. **This is the heart of the report. The depth here is what separates a real GTM from a pitch deck.**

## 8. Pricing Strategy & Analysis

> **Purpose:** Set a price that captures the value you create, fits the portfolio, survives competitive response, and clears breakeven. Price is the single most powerful profit lever — treat it analytically, not by gut.

### 8.1 Pricing Objective

> **How to complete:** State what price is optimizing *for* before you pick a number: market penetration (price low to win share), profit maximization (price to the value), portfolio consistency, or premium/skim positioning. The objective dictates the method.

`[[Pricing objective and why it fits the client's stage and goals.]]`

### 8.2 Price Point(s)

> **How to complete:** State the price(s), per segment/variant, and the unit. Tie each back to its objective and value.

| Variant / segment | Price | Unit | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| `[[variant]]` | `[[$]]` | `[[per unit / seat / mo / dozen / project]]` | `[[value-based / cost-plus / competitive]]` |

### 8.3 Value-Based Pricing & Willingness-to-Pay

> **How to complete:** Justify the price with the **economic value to the customer (EVC)** — quantify what the buyer gains or saves vs. their next-best alternative, then price as a fraction of that value. The strongest pricing narratives do real arithmetic (the source report computed dollars saved per round of golf from fewer lost balls). Show: alternative's cost → value created → price → buyer's net gain → payback.

`[[The value math: next-best-alternative cost, value created/saved, your price, and the buyer's net benefit — with a simple worked example.]]`

### 8.4 Portfolio Pricing Analysis

> **How to complete:** Check the new price against the rest of the client's lineup so it slots logically into the price ladder (e.g., Premium / Performance / Value tiers). A price wildly outside the existing range needs an explicit "new category" justification.

`[[Where the new price sits in the portfolio ladder and why it's coherent.]]`

### 8.5 Competitor Pricing

> **How to complete:** Map competitor prices for equivalent value. Note where you're at parity, premium, or discount — and whether any competitor even offers a true substitute (sometimes you're creating a new price point with no direct comp, which is a position of strength).

| Competitor | Comparable offer | Price | Your relative position |
|---|---|---|---|
| `[[A]]` | `[[...]]` | `[[$]]` | `[[parity / premium / discount / no direct comp]]` |

### 8.6 Product Economics (Demand, Revenue, Elasticity)

> **How to complete (advanced):** If data allows, sketch the **demand curve**, the **total-revenue curve** (revenue peaks where elasticity = 1), and the **price elasticity** of demand. Elasticity tells you what happens to revenue when you move price: |elasticity| > 1 = elastic (cutting price raises revenue); < 1 = inelastic (raising price raises revenue). Even rough estimates discipline the pricing decision.

`[[Demand/revenue/elasticity findings and what they imply for the optimal price.]]`

### 8.7 Breakeven Analysis

> **How to complete:** Compute breakeven for each price scenario. Formulas: **Contribution margin per unit = Price − Variable cost.** **Breakeven units = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin.** **Breakeven $ = Breakeven units × Price.** Run ≥3 scenarios (low / target / high price) and compare required volume against a realistic, projected volume so the reader sees the safety margin.

## 9. Competitive Game Theory

> **Purpose:** Stress-test the strategy against intelligent competitors. Strategy is interactive — anticipate the response, then plan your counter, before you commit.

> **How to complete:** Pick the 1–2 most likely competitive moves (new entrant copying you; an incumbent cutting price; a bundle response). Game each out using three principles: **Commitment** (what irreversible move locks in your advantage — e.g., an exclusive partnership or strategic control point), **Credible Threat** (what response you can believably make), and **Order Matters** (first-mover vs. fast-follower dynamics). Crucially: decide your response to a **price attack in advance** — the disciplined default is to **not** start a price war but to respond with a non-price "blunt instrument" (advertising surge, sales-incentive push, exclusivity, a marquee event).

**Scenario: `[[e.g., new competitor enters / incumbent undercuts price]]`**

- **Our commitment / control point:** `[[the locked-in advantage, e.g., exclusive supply or tech partnership, proprietary data, distribution lock]]`
- **Likely competitor move:** `[[what they do and when]]`
- **Our planned response:** `[[the counter — emphasize non-price levers; explain why not a price war]]`
- **Expected outcome:** `[[where this nets out]]`

## 11. Sales Motion

> **Purpose:** Distribution (§10) defines *where* customers can buy; this section defines *how the company actually sells and closes*. It is the revenue engine — the repeatable, end-to-end system that turns a target segment and a value prop into closed, expanding revenue. **This is the operating manual a client could hand to a new head of sales.**
>
> **How to complete:** Work the four sub-sections in order — Model → Process → Team → Tooling & Metrics — because each depends on the one before it. The motion must be *consistent with everything upstream*: the segment (§6), the price/ACV (§8), the access points (§10), and the channel mix (§12). A $50/mo self-serve product and a $250k enterprise deal demand opposite motions; the diagnostic in §11.1 keeps you honest.

### 11.1 Sales Model & Motion Type

> **How to complete:** First diagnose which motion the economics demand, then document the specifics. The cardinal rule: **the cost to acquire must fit the value of what's acquired.** Match the motion to the ACV and complexity below, then fill in the deal economics.

**Step 1 — Pick the motion archetype**

| Motion | Typical ACV | Sales cycle | Who drives the buy | Use when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Self-serve / PLG** | `< ~$5k` | minutes–days | The user (buys themselves) | Low price, fast time-to-value, individual/team adoption, viral or bottoms-up |

| **Enterprise / field** | `> ~$100k` | 3–12+ months | Formal buying committee | Complex, multi-threaded, security/legal/procurement, custom terms |
| **Channel / partner-led** | varies | varies | A reseller/VAR/SI or marketplace | Partner owns the relationship or fulfillment; you sell *through* them |

## 12. Channel Strategy & Mix (Selecting the Best GTM)

> **Purpose:** Decide the specific set of acquisition channels — and the motion-to-channel fit — that will reach and win this client's target segments. The neighboring sections answer different questions: §10 Distribution = *where the transaction happens*; §11 Sales Motion = *how we convert*; §13 Promotion = *the brand and message that run across channels*. **This section answers the one the others don't: of all the ways to reach a buyer — cold email, cold call, social, events, webinars, content/SEO, paid, partnerships, referrals, product-led — which mix is best for *this* client, and why?** The output is a ranked, resourced channel plan, not a list.
>
> **How to complete:** (1) Skim the channel menu (§12.1). (2) Score the candidates against this client's ICP, ACV, buyer behavior, and motion in the selection matrix (§12.2). (3) Commit to a focused mix — typically 1–2 primary, 1–2 secondary, 1–2 experiments — and state what you're deliberately *not* doing (§12.3). (4) Sequence and resource it (§12.4), then add per-channel playbooks (§12.5) and economics (§12.6).
>
> **The one rule that matters most:** Don't run eight channels at 10% effort each. Find one channel that reliably produces pipeline, saturate it, *then* layer the next. Concentration beats sprawl, especially early.

> 🔀 **Model note:** The best channels differ sharply by model. **Low-ACV / self-serve:** content/SEO, paid, organic social, community, product-led/referral. **Mid-ACV B2B:** outbound (cold email/call/social), webinars, paid, partnerships. **High-ACV / enterprise:** ABM, field events, executive & referral networks, partner co-sell.
