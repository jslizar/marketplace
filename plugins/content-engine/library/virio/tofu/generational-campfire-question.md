# Generational campfire question

- **Archetype**: Shared-identity observation ending in an "are we the only ones?" question
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — earns reach with the ICP; client-agnostic on the surface.
- **Stage mechanic**: Names a generational/tribal experience the ICP lived through; commenting = claiming membership. The closing in-group wink ("you are one of my people") converts nostalgia into follows.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: exactly 2, refreshed by the scheduled library-refresh task from the viral_posts_all corpus.
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/max-penk_when-i-was-young-i-fixed-my-parents-computers-activity-7253701040980602880-1D_5 — Max Penk, 170,147 reactions / 12,077 comments, 2024-10-20
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/annaclaiborne_when-i-was-young-i-fixed-computers-for-everyone-activity-7298084695185965056-w2w5 — Anna Claiborne, 641 reactions / 147 comments, 2025-02-19
- **Hook pattern**: "When I was young I {did X for the older generation}. Now I {do X for the younger one}. Are we the only generation that {skill/trait}?"
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Two-beat sandwich observation (young → old, same role)
  2. The question that names the tribe
  3. Honest evidence paragraph (tried and failed to pass it on)
  4. The thesis, softened with "maybe"
  5. In-group wink tied to an attached artifact/image
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Under 120 words. Conversational. An attached image that only the tribe decodes doubles the mechanic.
- **Why it works**: Identity-claim engagement — the comment section becomes the content. The "maybe" keeps it debatable rather than preachy, which sustains the thread.
- **Adaptation notes**: Pick the shared experience of the client's ICP cohort (first-gen sysadmins, pre-cloud marketers, spreadsheet-native finance). The failure-to-pass-it-on paragraph is what makes it feel true, not smug.
- **Watch-outs**: Punching at other generations flips it from campfire to flame war. Without a genuinely shared experience it reads as forced nostalgia.

## Top example — full post text

When I was young I fixed my parents computers. Now that I'm older I fix computers for my son & his friends. Are we the only generation that knows how computers work?

Yes, I tried to teach the young generation as well, but their interest didn't really spark when we installed a new GPU or took a look at BIOS settings.
Maybe we are the only practitioners of tech. The previous generation missed it, and the newer generation is just consumers of tech.
