# Stay-on-the-bus parable

- **Archetype**: parable-lesson
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — story-first reach, career-truth payoff, no ask.
- **Stage mechanic**: Borrows a named third-party parable (real person, real speech), walks it beat by beat, then translates it into the reader's career with a second mini-metaphor and a repeatable command.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: (this week's scan, second pass — author-seeded)
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sahilbloom_the-helsinki-bus-station-theory-activity-7482765919182577664-q4oB — Sahil Bloom, 414 reactions / 273 comments, 2026-07-14 (scanned 2026-07-16)
- **Hook pattern**: "Your {desired quality} is hiding behind your {vice}.\n\n(Stop {vice behavior}.)"
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Two-line accusation hook with parenthetical command
  2. Set the parable scene (named person, year, real event)
  3. Walk the thought experiment in short beats
  4. Map it to the reader's career ("You pick your platform…")
  5. "Here's what most people miss" turn + the divergence insight
  6. Quote the parable's punchline verbatim
  7. Second, faster metaphor reinforcing compounding (two pots of water)
  8. Restate the law, close on the command ("Stay on the bus.")
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Story beats in 1–2 line paragraphs; the quoted punchline carries the profanity/energy so the author doesn't have to. ~300 words.
- **Why it works**: Borrowed credibility (a real commencement speech) plus a sticky physical image makes an old lesson (consistency compounds) feel discovered, not preached.
- **Watch-outs**: The parable must be real and attributable. Two metaphors is the ceiling — a third dilutes.

## Top example — full post text

```
Your originality is hiding behind your impatience.

(Stop starting over.)

In 2004, a Finnish-American photographer named Arno Minkkinen gave the commencement speech at the New England School of Photography.

In the speech, he offered a powerful thought experiment:

Imagine for a moment that you find yourself at the large bus station in the center of Helsinki.

Dozens of platforms. Several lines per platform. And for the first few miles, every bus follows the exact same route.

He compared this to the early years of a career.

You pick your platform. You hop on a bus. Time passes.

Then you realize the work you're doing looks exactly like everyone else's. Same ideas. Same output. Nothing unique.

So you get off. Grab a cab back to the station. Find a new line.

A few years later, it happens again. You hop off. Race back. Start over.

Always chasing originality. Never staying long enough to find it.

Here's what most people miss:

The bus lines eventually diverge.

The routes that looked identical for the first few stops begin to separate. Each heading to its own unique destination.

Minkkinen's conclusion was simple: "Stay on the bus. Stay on the f*cking bus."

Picture two identical pots of water. One on the counter. One on a hot stove.

For a few minutes, they look exactly the same.

Then suddenly, one is boiling over while the other sits still.

The energy was compounding invisibly the entire time.

Originality works the same way.

You don't wake up singular. It's earned through long, uncomfortable periods of seemingly unoriginal work.

The boring, derivative work is where the beautiful, singular work begins.

The divergence is coming. You just can't see it yet.

Stay on the bus.
```
