# Absurd scene anecdote

- **Archetype**: scene-anecdote
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — a single vivid scene carries a worldview; no ask.
- **Stage mechanic**: Opens mid-absurdity (pitched a startup during a driving test), plays the scene straight with escalating reveals, then zooms out to the thesis the scene proves (SF's ambient ambition). The story is the argument.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: (this week's scan, second pass — author-seeded, Millies cohort)
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ericlay-virio_i-got-pitched-a-startup-in-the-middle-of-activity-7481014325671845888-zlT1 — Eric Lay (Virio), 351 reactions / 51 comments, 2026-07-09 (scanned 2026-07-16)
- **Hook pattern**: "{Absurd event} in the middle of {mundane ritual}.\n\nThe {unexpected actor}: {identity reveal}."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Two-line absurd hook with the reveal held to line two
  2. Deadpan setup (why the narrator was there)
  3. The scene escalates in beats, each reveal upping the stakes
  4. The topper reveal (instructor's daughter is a VC operating partner)
  5. One-line normalization ("Nobody in this city would find that strange.")
  6. Zoom out to the thesis + callback to a prior belief
  7. Personal stakes close ("San Francisco, I'm staying.")
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Screenplay pacing — short beats, present-tense mid-scene moments ("I'm mid parallel park"), no bullets. ~200 words.
- **Why it works**: The absurdity earns the read, the reveals keep it, and the thesis lands as the reader's own conclusion rather than a claim.
- **Watch-outs**: The scene must be true and specific; a generic "I met someone interesting" version has no engine. The thesis stays to one paragraph — more turns it into an op-ed.

## Top example — full post text

```
I got pitched a startup in the middle of my driving test.

The founder: my 50-year-old DMV instructor.

I didn't own a car, so I rented a Corolla from a company whose entire business is renting cars to adults taking the test later than everyone else. It came with an instructor to get me ready.

Ten minutes into the exam, he clocked that I'm a founder. In between giving me the exam, he was pitching me his company.

His startup: a marketplace that helps immigrants learn to drive in the US. He wanted to know if investors would back it. I'm mid parallel park and he's walking me through the model. Then he mentions his daughter. Ten years as an operating partner at a top VC firm.

My driving instructor is raising for his startup and has a direct line into venture. Nobody in this city would find that strange.

I once posted that every ambitious founder should spend three months in SF just to see what "possible" looks like. This is what I meant. The energy here is infectious. Everyone is chasing something. A 50-year-old instructor sees a founder in his passenger seat and takes his shot. I respect it. I crossed the world twice for that same energy.

San Francisco, I'm staying. Glad I made it. Again.
```
