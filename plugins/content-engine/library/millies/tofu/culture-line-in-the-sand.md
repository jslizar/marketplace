# Culture line-in-the-sand

- **Archetype**: polarizing culture manifesto that deliberately filters the audience
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — earns reach through argument; the comment war IS the distribution.
- **Stage mechanic**: Takes one contested norm (996, remote work, work-life balance) and plants a flag on the spicy side, framed through the company's actual practice. Half the ICP amplifies in agreement, half in outrage — both spread it. Doubles as a self-selecting hiring filter.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sugju-choi_shove-your-job-up-your-azz-worst-and-best-activity-7407476497453809664-7-DF — Sugi Choi (Virio), 591 reactions / 372 comments, 2025-12-18
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/james-k-he_we-at-artificial-societies-dont-believe-activity-7378391641730023424-NDkB — James He (Artificial Societies), 291 reactions / 44 comments, 2025-10-08
- **Hook pattern**: Quoted outrage or inverted cliché: "\"SHOVE YOUR JOB UP YOUR AZZ\" — worst and best line I heard from a candidate." / "We at {company} don't believe in 996 — we only work when we want to work."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Polarizing hook (a quote, a heresy, or a both-sides paradox)
  2. Name the practice plainly, no euphemism ("yes, we do 996, 9am-9pm, 6 days")
  3. Stage the two reactions the post will get (pre-empting the comments)
  4. The filter logic: who this is for, who it isn't, and why that's the point
  5. Honest concession beat (the cost, the caveat — "we know this isn't sustainable forever")
  6. Self-selection close ("if you read this and thought X — we should talk") — follow-level CTA max
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Punchy short paragraphs, quoted dialogue rendered as blockquote-style lines, 200–400 words.
- **Why it works**: Controversy with receipts. Staging both reactions inoculates against the backlash while feeding it. The concession beat converts "unhinged" into "honest," which is what makes it shareable by serious people.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: the norm being contested and the company practice (must be TRUE — this format is a public commitment). Must NOT change: the pre-staged reactions and the concession beat; without them it's just rage-bait.
- **Watch-outs**: Client must actually live the policy — the comments will test it. Check the client's Canon and any HR/legal exposure before drafting. Never punch down.

## Top example — full post text

"SHOVE YOUR JOB UP YOUR AZZ"

Worst and best line I heard from a candidate I interviewed. Yes, we do 996 which means working 9am-9pm, 6 days a week.

It's funny because there are two extremes of how people react when I say that. They either say:

> "you're a slave, a victim of capitalism and hustle culture, your CEO is exploiting you!!! shove that job up your azz"

or:

> "oh man I do that already lol"

The reason why we keep it transparent is because we're looking for those people.

People who've run their own business or started their own startup get it. Building something from nothing is extremely hard. It demands sacrifice, obsession, and an unreasonable amount of hours.

That doesn't mean you're literally chained to your desk for 12 hours a day. It means putting in your best effort - 100%, 120%. But saying "996" is the best filter.

We're not trying to convince anyone this lifestyle is for them. If your gut reaction is the first one - respect. Genuinely. That boundary is valid.
But if you read 996 and thought "yeah that's just Tuesday" - we should talk.

And look - we know this isn't sustainable forever. As the team grows, the pace will change. 996 is an early-stage reality, not a long-term culture. The goal is to earn the right to slow down by building something that works first.
