# Founder reintroduction

- **Archetype**: milestone-triggered "let me reintroduce myself" life story
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — earns reach with the ICP by making the founder a character worth following.
- **Stage mechanic**: A cold ICP member shares it because the origin story is genuinely surprising (quant trading, name changes, silent retreats) — the company ride-along is incidental. Follows accrue to the person, which the brand later converts.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.

- **Hook pattern**: "After being head-down for {n} years building {company} and {credibility stat}, I think it's time to reintroduce myself." / "I haven't posted much here, but that's about to change. Let me reintroduce myself."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Milestone hook + "reintroduce myself" declaration
  2. "Hi, my name is {name}" beat (disarmingly plain)
  3. 3–5 origin-story chapters, each with one hyper-specific detail (a number, a place, a failure)
  4. The turn: the lesson that led to the current company
  5. Present-day proof block (what we've built, one stat)
  6. Forward-looking close ("here's what you can expect from me") — no CTA
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Long-form (400–600 words), short paragraphs, one-line beats between chapters. Specific nouns everywhere; zero adjectives doing the work.
- **Why it works**: Curiosity gap (who IS this person) + specificity as credibility + the reintroduction frame gives long-time lurkers permission to engage. The milestone (raise, anniversary) makes it news, not navel-gazing.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: every biographical chapter (real details only — this format dies with invented color). Must NOT change: the plain "Hi, my name is" beat and the milestone trigger; without a why-now it reads as vanity.
- **Watch-outs**: Highlight-reel syndrome — chapters with no failure or weirdness read as a resume. Needs 4+ verifiable specifics or it's dead.

## Top example — full post text

After being head-down for the last 4 years building Wispr and raising over $81M in funding, I think it's time to reintroduce myself.

Hi, my name is Sahaj.

Before building Wispr, I spent my sophomore year of undergrad at Stanford treating research like a mini-PhD. 50 hours a week in the lab while neglecting most of my actual coursework.

What was I spending these 50 hours a week on? Researching and writing one of the earliest papers at Stanford on diffusion-based generative models.

Back then, we had this bet: if you could represent image data in a more intuitive way, the model would be way easier to train and work better.

It barely worked when we were building it. Now it's the foundation of every major image generation model.

From Jersey kid doing math competitions and neuroscience research on rhesus macaques, to quant trading (hated it - people coin-flipped $20 bills for fun because it nets zero), to Google ML fairness work that's now deployed in YouTube.

Between undergrad quarters, I walked the Camino de Santiago, 500 miles across northern Spain. Did a 10-day silent Vipassana retreat.

The big takeaway: learning via experience, wisdom not just knowledge, is the only way to actually understand anything.

That pushed me to join my first startup.

Optical computing company, $100M raised, eventually shut down. Learned a critical lesson: I could see the product was off track but wasn't senior enough to make the case for pivoting.

Coming out of that, I knew what mattered: solving a problem I actually care about, with someone I love working with. [...]

Looking forward to the next 4 years with our amazing team
