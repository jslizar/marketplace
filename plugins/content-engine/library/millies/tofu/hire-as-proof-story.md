# Hire-as-proof story

- **Archetype**: one specific hire narrated as evidence of how the company thinks
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — earns reach with the ICP; recruiting-brand story with a business-lesson spine.
- **Stage mechanic**: The unusual hire (a linguistics PhD, a Palantir defector, a first Head of Sales) is inherently curious; the narration converts curiosity into a lesson about standards, which operators share as management wisdom.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.

- **Hook pattern**: "We just hired {company}'s first {role}. Here's an inside look at {the process/what happened}." / "We hired a {unexpected credential} who {unexpected specialty}. Within {timeframe}, {concrete result}."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. The hire, stated with its surprising element + a concrete payoff number if available
  2. Why this hire was hard/unusual (what they turned down, what you tested for)
  3. The process or test, in real detail (the exercise, the interviews, the criteria)
  4. "But here's where it gets interesting" turn — what the hire did after joining
  5. The principle: what this says about how you hire/build — no CTA (the post recruits by itself)
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Story-forward, short paragraphs, 1–2 arrow-bullet blocks for criteria. 250–450 words.
- **Why it works**: A single named human is more shareable than a hiring policy. The payoff number ("saves us $100k+/month") converts a feel-good story into a management case study.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: the person, the test, the numbers (all real, with the hire's consent). Must NOT change: the mid-story turn — a hire story without a post-joining twist is a welcome post, not this format.
- **Watch-outs**: Get the featured person's sign-off. If the "unusual" element is thin, the post reads as HR content — the credential/defection must genuinely surprise.

## Top example — full post text

We just hired Clay's first Head of Sales. Here's an inside look at what the interview process looked like (and the take-home prompts I think other teams might find helpful):

As you can imagine, I know a lot of sales leaders! In fact, it's my day job to meet them. So you can believe me when I say I knew Becca Lindquist was special the first time we met.

Let's start with what I was looking for:

1. Philosophical alignment: We approach sales differently at Clay. We hire GTM engineers to sell Clay in a highly consultative process that prizes product knowledge and customer empathy over traditional sales backgrounds. We have a low-pressure, fit-oriented approach.

2. People leadership and process: Our org is scaling quickly, and we need someone to come in and build a system that can help us scale, not just win based off force of will and charisma.

Here was the interview process:

First, I set up multiple behavioral interviews for Becca on sales process and people management with sales leaders at customers we trusted (thank you Dannie Herzberg and Stevie Case!). Then we did a deep dive interview where we went through every role in her career in great detail. We also asked her to complete a take-home with the following instructions:

- Current State: We'll share Gong calls, written docs, closed/won + pipeline data, and would love to see what you come back with in terms of top 5 opp[ortunities...]
