# Counterintuitive-investment story

- **Archetype**: "we spent big on the thing nobody spends on" reveal with the reasoning receipts
- **Funnel stage**: TOFU — earns reach with the ICP; a values statement disguised as an operations story.
- **Stage mechanic**: The decision violates startup orthodoxy in a direction that signals seriousness (air quality over perks, staying cramped over upgrading). Founders/operators share it as a debate prompt about priorities.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.

- **Hook pattern**: Confession or contradiction in 1–2 stacked lines: "We were poisoning our employees, and we didn't even know it." / "We raised $38M. Kept our cramped office. Hit 300% capacity."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Shock/contradiction hook
  2. The overlooked problem or expectation everyone holds, with one hard number (CO₂ ppm, capacity %)
  3. The mechanism: why the conventional choice is wrong (short, factual lines)
  4. The decision + named specifics (the building, the expert, the sq ft, the desk math)
  5. The anticipated objection voiced ("people ask me, why would a startup invest in this?")
  6. Values close: the one-line ROI logic ("A 30% cognitive gain is worth everything.") — no CTA
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Very short lines, heavy line breaks, numbers in almost every beat. 150–300 words.
- **Why it works**: Costly signaling — money spent against fashion is unfakeable proof of values. The named specifics (partners, addresses, figures) turn a brag into a document.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: the investment, the numbers, the named specifics — all verifiable from the client's Canon. Must NOT change: the voiced objection beat and the single-line ROI close.
- **Watch-outs**: Without real dollar/measurement specifics it collapses into "we care about our people" mush. If the investment is standard-issue (nice office, good laptops), there's no story.

## Top example — full post text

We raised $38M. Kept our cramped office. Hit 300% capacity. Now we're moving in a space we designed.

When we closed our Series B, everyone expected us to upgrade immediately.
We didn't.

We kept adding desks.
Squeezed in phone booths.
Put people wherever they fit.

At one point, we had 30 people and 1 bathroom.
We hit 300% over capacity.

Now we're moving into a space 5x larger.
Every detail we couldn't control before, we designed this time.

We didn't stay in the old office just to be scrappy.

It was about priorities.
Build the product.
Hire the team.
Then build the space.

We're ready now.
