# Stakeholder playbook

- **Archetype**: "I've done ${result}, here's exactly how" — numbered tactical playbook with mini-stories per tactic
- **Funnel stage**: MOFU — makes the approach credible via operating detail only a practitioner would know.
- **Stage mechanic**: The earned-result hook ("closed $7M in enterprise deals") buys the right to teach; each numbered tactic includes the actual script/artifact (quoted discovery lines, 10-K reads), which practitioners save and try tomorrow.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zhu-amanda_ive-now-closed-7m-in-enterprise-deals-activity-7413249035068841990-8lCZ — Amanda Zhu, 208 reactions / 12 comments, 2026-01-03
  - Candidate (unread, from Millie's bank): arlan-rakhmetzhanov "six things I've learned" year-end variant
- **Hook pattern**: "I've now {result with number}. Here's how I {the skill}, for each {unit}:"
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Result hook (number first, no context padding)
  2. 3–5 numbered tactics, each: name → the move in 2–3 lines → a verbatim artifact (quoted script, tool, document) → the outcome it produced
  3. Divider lines between tactics ("-----")
  4. One tactic carries a mini-case ("in one deal, I kept getting polite interest...") — the story beat
  5. Principle close: the through-line of all tactics — no hard CTA
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Numbered sections with dividers, quoted scripts rendered as dialogue, 300–450 words.
- **Why it works**: Verbatim artifacts (the actual sentence said to a stakeholder) are the difference between advice and a playbook. Saves-driven distribution; low comment count is fine — this format converts quietly.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: the result number and every tactic (from the client's real practice). Must NOT change: artifact-per-tactic rule — a tactic without its script is a platitude.
- **Watch-outs**: The result must be the client's own and Canon-verifiable. Generic tactics ("do your research") disqualify the draft — every tactic needs an artifact.

I've now closed $7M in enterprise deals. Here's how I personalize the pitch for each stakeholder:

1/ Talk to THEIR customers

Before the first pitch, I talk to people who already use their product.

Instead of selling features,
I use their users' words to diagnose their problem:

"I talked to a few of your customers,
and they mentioned that X was a pain.
What if there was a way to have [solution]?"

-----

2/ Reframe using analyst positioning

I read reports from Gartner, Forrester, or G2
to see who the buyer is being lumped in with.
Because every enterprise buyer has a comparison they hate.

I'll often anchor the conversation with:
"This gives you a concrete way to separate from X, Y, and Z when those comparisons come up."

This gets us talking about positioning, risk, and narrative,
which helps me uncover what the execs actually care about.

-----

3/ 10-K reports are your friend

A 10-K is the company telling you what matters this year.

In one deal, I kept getting polite interest,
but no urgency.

So before the next meeting, I read their most recent 10-K.

One thing jumped out:
they had explicitly committed to improving reliability as a top company priority.

In the next call, I honed in on how we can help with that.
That was the first time the conversation clicked.

Suddenly, we were tied to something leadership had already promised the market.
