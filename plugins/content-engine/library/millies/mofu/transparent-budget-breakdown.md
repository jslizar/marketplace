# Transparent budget breakdown

- **Archetype**: open-books line-item budget with the reasoning behind every allocation change
- **Funnel stage**: MOFU — credibility through radical transparency; evaluators benchmark against it.
- **Stage mechanic**: Real dollar allocations are information the ICP can't get elsewhere. Every line item ("$1.2M paid ads — LinkedIn CPMs up 30-40%") is a benchmarkable data point; practitioners save it, debate it, and screenshot it into their planning decks.
- **Platform**: LinkedIn
- **Source examples**: curated from Millie's bank; refreshed only when the bank updates.
  - https://www.linkedin.com/posts/madhavbhandari_were-spending-33m-on-marketing-in-2026-activity-7430219623037407232-AwZY — Madhav Bhandari (Storylane), 848 reactions / 191 comments, 2026-02-19
  - (Single-example spec — pin a second when Millie's bank supplies one.)
- **Hook pattern**: "We're spending ${total} on {function} in {year} at {company} (up {n}% from {last year})."
- **Skeleton** (block sequence):
  1. Total-number hook with year-over-year delta
  2. Transparency credo, one line ("I've always believed in being transparent... here's the full breakup")
  3. Line items: "→ ${amount} - {category}" each followed by 2–4 lines of what changed and WHY (cut from X, testing Y, bet on Z)
  4. Items ordered biggest-first; every item names a decision, not just a number
  5. (Optional) what-we're-NOT-spending-on beat — soft comment prompt close
- **Rhythm & formatting**: Arrow line items with dollar amounts, tight reasoning grafs under each. 300–500 words.
- **Why it works**: Costly disclosure — sharing real budgets is unfakeable insider generosity. The reasoning lines carry the strategy lesson; the dollar figures carry the shareability.
- **Adaptation notes**: Must change: every number and allocation (client's real budget, leadership-approved for disclosure). Must NOT change: amount-plus-reasoning pairing — numbers without the why is just a table.
- **Watch-outs**: Requires explicit client sign-off to publish financials. Competitors will read it — flag that tradeoff before drafting. Stale numbers (last year's budget) gut the format.
